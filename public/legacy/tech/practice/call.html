Tech Support Call: Part 1/3

You call the number on the popup. Listen to the conversation by clicking the button to the left. Then answer the questions that follow on the right based on what you heard.

  • Scammer: Thank you for calling tech support. How can I assist you today?
  • Target: Hi, I received a pop-up on my computer saying it's infected with a virus, and it won't go away. I'm really worried about my personal information being at risk.
  • Scammer: I understand your concern. This is a serious issue, and we need to take immediate action to protect your computer and data.
  • Target: Okay, what do I need to do?
  • Scammer: First, I'll need you to provide me with remote access to your computer so that I can run a diagnostic scan and identify the problem.

Click the buttons below to identify the warning signs related to this part of the conversation

Great Job! In this conversation, there are several warning signs to be aware of. First, unsolicited contact—the target receives a pop-up warning about a virus. Legitimate tech support companies won't contact you out of the blue asking to be called for help. The tech agent then creates a sense of pressure and urgency making you feel like you must act immediately to protect your computer, which is a tactic to make you panic and follow their instructions without thinking. Next there is a lack of verification—the tech agent doesn’t confirm the actual problem or help troubleshoot before resorting to extreme measures of requests for remote access. Once they obtain the remote access to your computer, they would have the ability to go anywhere and do anything on your computer without your knowledge or control.
